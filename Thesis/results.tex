\chapter{Quantum Dots}

We study two-dimensional harmonic quantum dots, a numerically delicate setting
due to the Coulomb singularity at short range and the second-order derivatives
in the kinetic term. Both effects can yield ill-conditioned objectives that
typically require many epochs and small learning rates. We consider
two-electron, six-electron and twelve-electron dots across a wide confinement
range ($\omega\!\in\!\{1.0,\,0.5,\,0.1,\,0.01,\,0.001\}$). 
When $\omega$ becomes sufficiently small, the system approaches the Wigner
molecule limit, where inter-electron distances become large compared to the
confinement length scale and the electrons localize into geometric
arrangements that minimize their mutual repulsion while balancing the trap
potential.
As $\omega$ decreases, the well broadens and correlations strengthen; 
nevertheless, the compact Slater--Jastrow PINN with soft-core pair features
and an explicit analytic cusp remains stable and accurate in practice.
In addition, we provide a detailed analysis of the learned representations,
focusing on the correlator manifold and the backflow field.


\section{Energy calculations}v
\label{sec:energy}

Table~\ref{tab:energies} reports ground-state energies obtained with residual-based pretraining followed by a
stochastic reconfiguration (SR) tail, for both PINN+BF and PINN+CTNN trial states.
Where available, we compare directly to diffusion Monte Carlo (DMC) reference values, and list the relative
deviation (\%\,err) from DMC.

For $N=2$, both ans\"atze reproduce the DMC benchmarks essentially at the level of the reported uncertainties
across the full confinement range $\omega\in[0.01,1]$. This indicates that, for the two-electron problem,
the optimization pipeline is able to find a near-optimal nodal/shape representation for the ground state in
all regimes considered.

For larger particle numbers, the comparison highlights a consistent performance difference between the two
ans\"atze in the externally benchmarked subset. For $N=6$ and $N=12$, PINN+CTNN agrees more closely with DMC
at moderate and strong confinement (e.g.\ $\omega=0.1,0.5,1.0$), while PINN+BF exhibits small but systematic
positive biases in several cases. For $N=20$, DMC comparisons at $\omega\in\{0.1,0.5,1.0\}$ show that the CTNN
ansatz remains accurate at scale, with the largest deviation appearing at $\omega=0.1$ and substantially smaller
deviations at $\omega=0.5$ and $\omega=1.0$.

For the weakest confinements ($\omega\in\{10^{-3},10^{-2}\}$) DMC references are not listed in Table~\ref{tab:energies}
for several $N$, and these energies should therefore be interpreted as internally consistent variational results
supporting the structural analysis below, rather than as externally benchmarked error statements.
To quantify this internal consistency, we additionally analyze the energy partitioning
\begin{equation}
E \;=\; \langle T\rangle + \langle V_{\rm int}\rangle + \langle V_{\rm trap}\rangle,
\qquad
V_{\rm trap}=\tfrac12 \omega^2 \sum_{i=1}^N \langle r_i^2\rangle,
\end{equation}
together with two diagnostics that directly probe the approach to the interaction-dominated (Wigner) regime:
\begin{equation}
\Gamma(\omega) \;=\;\frac{\langle V_{\rm int}\rangle}{\langle T\rangle},
\qquad
\mathcal C(\omega)\;=\;\frac{2\langle V_{\rm trap}\rangle}{\langle V_{\rm int}\rangle}.
\label{eq:gamma-classical}
\end{equation}

Across all reported $(N,\omega)$ the component estimators satisfy energy closure
$E-(T+V_{\rm int}+V_{\rm trap})\approx 0$ (typically $10^{-11}$--$10^{-7}$ in absolute units), and the
harmonic-plus-Coulomb virial identity
\begin{equation}
2\langle T\rangle \;=\; 2\langle V_{\rm trap}\rangle - \langle V_{\rm int}\rangle
\end{equation}
is satisfied to sub-percent relative accuracy (worst case $\lesssim 0.4\%$ in our dataset), providing a strong
physics-based validation of the low-$\omega$ variational estimates.



The ratio trends are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:energy_partitions} and Fig.~\ref{fig:energy_ratios}.
As $\omega$ decreases, $\Gamma$ increases sharply, e.g.\ from
$\Gamma\simeq 0.93\to 8.64$ for $N=2$ and $\Gamma\simeq 4.66\to 57.3$ for $N=20$ when going from $\omega=1$ to $\omega=10^{-3}$,
confirming a systematic crossover towards interaction-dominated states.
Simultaneously, $\mathcal C(\omega)$ approaches unity at weak confinement, improving markedly with particle number:
at $\omega=10^{-3}$ we find $\mathcal C\simeq 1.226$ ($N=2$), $1.074$ ($N=6$), $1.049$ ($N=12$), and $1.038$ ($N=20$),
so that already at $N\ge 12$ the dot obeys $V_{\rm int}\approx 2V_{\rm trap}$ to within $\sim 5\%$ at $\omega=10^{-3}$.

From $\langle V_{\rm trap}\rangle$ we also extract a root-mean-square radius per particle,
\begin{equation}
r_{\rm rms} \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{2\langle V_{\rm trap}\rangle}{N\omega^2}},
\label{eq:rrms}
\end{equation}
which expands rapidly as confinement weakens (Fig.~\ref{fig:energy_ratios}c).
For $\omega=1$ we obtain $r_{\rm rms}\approx 1.14,\,1.62,\,2.04,\,2.40$ for $N=2,6,12,20$, respectively,
whereas at $\omega=10^{-3}$ the corresponding values are $\approx 70,\,126,\,171,\,209$,
consistent with strong interaction-driven swelling in the Wigner regime.

\chapter{Representation Geometry and Backflow Diagnostics}
\label{ch:repgeom}

\section{What we measure and why}
\label{sec:repgeom_metrics}

Having established that both the PINN and CTNN-backflow ans\"atze achieve accurate ground-state energies
(Table~\ref{tab:energies}), we now ask a deeper question: \emph{what structure do the learned representations
encode, and how does this structure change across confinement regimes?} This analysis serves two purposes.
First, it provides interpretability---connecting the abstract neural network features to physical quantities
like pair correlations, angular order, and cusp behavior. Second, it validates that the model learns physically
meaningful representations rather than exploiting spurious correlations.

We organize our analysis around three complementary perspectives:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Dimensionality}: How many effective degrees of freedom does the representation use?
\item \textbf{Geometry}: What is the structure of the backflow displacement field---radial vs.\ tangential,
near-field vs.\ far-field?
\item \textbf{Feature importance}: Which input features (coordinates, angles, pair distances) drive the
learned corrections, and how does this change with $\omega$?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Dimensionality metrics}

We employ several complementary notions of ``dimensionality'' that capture different aspects of the representation.
These are not redundant: some measure \emph{variance dispersion} in an ambient feature space, while others attempt
to estimate the \emph{intrinsic manifold dimension} actually explored across sampled configurations.

\paragraph{Effective rank (variance dispersion).}
Given a feature matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times F}$ (batch of $B$ configurations, feature dimension $F$),
let $\sigma_i$ be its singular values and $p_i = \sigma_i / \sum_j \sigma_j$.
The \emph{effective rank} is the exponential of the Shannon entropy:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{erank}(X) = \exp\!\Big(-\sum_i p_i \log p_i\Big).
\end{equation}
It measures variance dispersion: $\mathrm{erank} \approx 1$ when variance concentrates in one direction,
and $\mathrm{erank} \approx F$ when variance spreads uniformly.

\paragraph{Intrinsic dimension (local manifold estimate).}
We estimate the intrinsic manifold dimension $\mathrm{idim}$ using local neighborhood statistics, which
reflects how many degrees of freedom the representation actually explores (as opposed to the ambient dimension).
For $N=2$, this estimator can be ill-conditioned due to the small ambient space, so we focus on $N \geq 6$.

\paragraph{PCA variance concentration.}
We report $\mathrm{PC1Var}$, the variance explained by the first principal component, and $k_{95}$, the number
of components needed to capture 95\% of variance. Large $\mathrm{PC1Var}$ indicates a strong dominant mode;
small values indicate more isotropic/complex variation.

\paragraph{Two kinds of ``dimensionality'' can disagree on purpose.}
It is normal (and physically meaningful) to see $\mathrm{idim}$ remain small while PCA-based measures (erank,
$k_{95}$, PR-dimension) are large: a low-dimensional manifold can curve through many ambient directions and thus require
many PCA modes to capture its variance. We exploit this distinction below rather than treating it as a contradiction.

\subsection{Backflow geometry metrics}

\paragraph{Near-field amplification.}
To test whether the backflow correction emphasizes close electron encounters (cusp physics), we define:
\begin{equation}
A_{\rm nf}(q)=
\frac{\mathbb{E}\big[\|\Delta x\|^2 \mid r_{ij} \le Q_q(r)\big]}
{\mathbb{E}\big[\|\Delta x\|^2\big]},
\end{equation}
where $Q_q(r)$ is the $q$-quantile of pair distances. Values $A_{\rm nf} > 1$ indicate that the backflow
displacement is amplified during close encounters.

\paragraph{Radial vs.\ tangential decomposition.}
For each particle, we decompose the relative (center-of-mass-free) backflow displacement into components
parallel to local pair directions (radial, $f_{\rm rad}$) and orthogonal to them (tangential, $f_{\rm tan}$).
A radial-dominated correction ($f_{\rm rad} \gg f_{\rm tan}$) indicates ``push-apart'' physics responding
to Coulomb repulsion. A tangential-dominated correction indicates collective angular/shear rearrangements.

\paragraph{Center-of-mass content.}
We verify that the center-of-mass (COM) component of the leading backflow mode is negligible
($\mathrm{PC1}_{\rm COM} \approx 0$), confirming that the learned correction is purely \emph{relative}
motion---a physics-consistent sanity check, since the Hamiltonian is translationally invariant.

\paragraph{Linear CKA alignment.}
We compute a linear centered kernel alignment score between the PINN intermediate representation $\rho_{\rm in}$
and the backflow field $\Delta x$, denoted $\mathrm{CKA}(\rho_{\rm in},\Delta x)$. Values near zero indicate
little \emph{linear} correspondence; this does \emph{not} rule out nonlinear dependence.


\section{Global trends across $N$ and $\omega$}
\label{sec:repgeom_global_trends}

Table~\ref{tab:repgeom_casegrid} summarizes the main diagnostics across all $(N, \omega)$ combinations.
Several robust patterns emerge.

\subsection{The PINN output collapses; the backflow field is high-dimensional}

\paragraph{(i) The PINN output representation collapses to an effectively 1D object.}
Across all cases we find the \emph{final} PINN output representation ($\rho_{\rm out}$, not shown in the table to save
space) to be essentially rank-one: $\mathrm{erank}(\rho_{\rm out}) \approx 1$, $\mathrm{idim}(\rho_{\rm out}) \approx 1$,
and $\mathrm{PC1Var}(\rho_{\rm out}) \approx 1$.
This is expected if the last stage is a scalar head (energy/residual channel) and is a useful sanity check: the last map
compresses the representation to a nearly one-dimensional decision variable.

\paragraph{(ii) The backflow field is genuinely high-dimensional and scales strongly with $N$.}
In contrast, the learned displacement field $\Delta x\in\mathbb{R}^{2N}$ has large effective rank that grows with $N$
(Table~\ref{tab:repgeom_Navg}). For $N=20$, $\mathrm{erank}(\Delta x)$ reaches the mid-30s (out of an ambient $2N=40$),
and $\mathrm{idim}(\Delta x)$ is in the low 20s, indicating a highly structured but still strongly constrained vector field.

This scaling has an important interpretation: the learned backflow field is \emph{not} a simple low-mode
correction (e.g., a uniform breathing mode or rigid rotation). Instead, it encodes a genuinely high-dimensional
correlated displacement field that grows in complexity with the number of electrons. The ratio
$\mathrm{idim}(\Delta x) / 2N \approx 0.55$--$0.75$ suggests that roughly half to three-quarters of the
configuration-space degrees of freedom are actively modulated by the backflow.

\subsection{Regime dependence: the intermediate-$\omega$ ``competition regime''}

Averaging over $N \geq 6$ (Table~\ref{tab:repgeom_omegaavg}), the intermediate confinement regime
$\omega \approx 0.1$ stands out as geometrically richest:
\begin{itemize}
\item The PINN input representation shows elevated complexity: $\langle\mathrm{erank}(\rho_{\rm in})\rangle = 2.78$
and $\langle\mathrm{idim}(\rho_{\rm in})\rangle = 5.09$, compared to $\sim 2.4$ and $\sim 4.5$ at $\omega = 1$.
\item The tangential fraction peaks: $\langle f_{\rm tan}\rangle = 0.55$ at $\omega = 0.1$, versus
$\langle f_{\rm tan}\rangle = 0.42$ at $\omega = 1$.
\item Near-field amplification is largest: $\langle A_{\rm nf}\rangle = 1.73$ at $\omega = 0.1$.
\end{itemize}

This pattern is physically intuitive. At strong confinement ($\omega \gtrsim 1$), kinetic energy dominates
and the system resembles a weakly-correlated Fermi gas; backflow corrections are modest. At very weak
confinement ($\omega \lesssim 0.01$), the system approaches the Wigner limit with strong localization;
correlations are intense but geometrically ``simple'' (rigid shell structures). The intermediate regime
is where neither picture applies cleanly, and the network must learn complex angular correlations and
collective modes that interpolate between these limits.

\subsection{Radial vs.\ tangential content}

The decomposition of backflow into radial and tangential components reveals a clear physical story
(Table~\ref{tab:repgeom_casegrid}, column $f_{\rm tan}$).

For $N = 2$, the tangential fraction is essentially zero ($f_{\rm tan} \approx 0$). This is expected:
with only two electrons, there is no meaningful ``tangential'' direction---any correlation correction
must be along the inter-particle axis.

For $N \geq 6$, substantial tangential content emerges. The $\omega$-averaged values are
$\langle f_{\rm tan}\rangle = 0.62$ for $N=6$, $0.50$ for $N=12$, and $0.35$ for $N=20$.
The decrease with $N$ likely reflects the increasing importance of radial shell structure in larger
systems, but even at $N=20$ more than one-third of the backflow energy is tangential.

The regime dependence is equally informative. For $N=6$, the tangential fraction peaks at
$f_{\rm tan} = 0.70$ for $\omega = 0.01$---precisely the regime where the system transitions
between compact and extended configurations. This indicates that angular/shear rearrangements
are most important in the crossover region.

\subsection{Near-field amplification and cusp physics}

The near-field amplification $A_{\rm nf}$ (evaluated at the 5th percentile of pair distances)
tests whether the backflow correction emphasizes close electron encounters.

For $N \geq 6$, we consistently find $A_{\rm nf} > 1$, with the strongest amplification at
intermediate confinement: $A_{\rm nf} = 2.72$ for $(N=6, \omega=0.1)$ and $A_{\rm nf} = 2.68$
for $(N=6, \omega=0.01)$. This confirms that the learned backflow systematically responds to
close-encounter events, consistent with the need to handle Coulomb cusp physics.

Interestingly, $A_{\rm nf}$ decreases for larger $N$ at fixed $\omega$: the $\omega$-averaged
values are $\langle A_{\rm nf}\rangle = 1.92$ for $N=6$, $1.25$ for $N=12$, and $0.78$ for $N=20$.
This likely reflects the decreasing probability of close encounters in larger, more extended systems,
rather than reduced importance of cusp corrections per se.

\subsection{Alignment between $\rho_{\rm in}$ and $\Delta x$ is weakly linear}

The linear CKA alignment between $\rho_{\rm in}$ and $\Delta x$ remains small (order $10^{-2}$),
confirming that the backflow is not a trivial linear readout of the PINN features. The relationship
is fundamentally nonlinear.

More interesting is the \emph{input} representation $\rho_{\rm in}$, which feeds into the CTNN
backflow network. Its effective rank remains modest ($\mathrm{erank}(\rho_{\rm in}) \approx 2$--$3$)
despite the much higher dimensionality of the backflow it helps generate. This suggests that the
CTNN performs substantial nonlinear expansion from the PINN features.

\section{Feature-level interpretability}
\label{sec:feature_importance}

Beyond dimensionality and geometry, we can ask: \emph{which input features drive the learned representations?}
We address this through ablation studies and correlation analysis, revealing how the network allocates
capacity across different physical quantities.

\subsection{Feature ablation importance}

We measure feature importance by computing the increase in reconstruction error when specific feature
groups are ablated (zeroed out). Table~\ref{tab:feature_ablation} summarizes the ablation importance
for three feature categories: angular features ($\phi$), pair-angle features ($\psi$), and coordinate
features ($x$).

The regime dependence is striking:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Weak confinement} ($\omega \leq 0.01$): Angular and pair-angle features dominate with large
ablation scores. For $(N=12, \omega=0.001)$, we find $\mathrm{abl}_\phi = 99.2$, $\mathrm{abl}_\psi = 39.9$,
and $\mathrm{abl}_x = 39.8$. For $(N=20, \omega=0.001)$, the pair-angle importance explodes to
$\mathrm{abl}_\psi = 1222$.
\item \textbf{Intermediate confinement} ($\omega \approx 0.1$): Pair-angle features become dominant.
For $(N=12, \omega=0.1)$, $\mathrm{abl}_\psi = 182.6$ dominates, with $\mathrm{abl}_\phi = 1.3$ and
$\mathrm{abl}_x = 11.8$. This indicates that \emph{relative} angular correlations between electron pairs
are most important in the competition regime.
\item \textbf{Strong confinement} ($\omega \geq 0.5$): All ablation importances decrease substantially.
For $(N=12, \omega=1)$, $\mathrm{abl}_\psi = 32.5$, $\mathrm{abl}_\phi = 0.8$, $\mathrm{abl}_x = 1.6$.
\end{itemize}

This pattern connects directly to the physics. In the Wigner regime, electrons localize at well-defined
angular positions on shells, making absolute angles $\phi$ critical for larger systems. The pair-angle
features $\psi$ capture \emph{relative} angular correlations between electron pairs, which are most
important in the crossover and intermediate regimes. At strong confinement, the compact, delocalized
wavefunction requires less angular structure overall.

\subsection{Energy--feature correlations}

We compute Pearson correlations between individual PINN features and the local energy estimator.
The maximum absolute correlation $|\mathrm{corr}|_{\max}$ across features provides a measure of
how directly the representation encodes energy-relevant information (Table~\ref{tab:energy_corr}).

The regime dependence reveals different representational strategies:
\begin{itemize}
\item At strong confinement ($\omega = 1$): Individual features can directly encode energy-relevant
quantities. For $N=2$, $|\mathrm{corr}|_{\max} = 0.88$---a remarkably direct encoding. For larger
systems the correlation is weaker but still substantial ($0.21$--$0.41$).
\item At weak confinement ($\omega = 0.001$): The mapping from features to energy becomes more
distributed. For $N=2$, $|\mathrm{corr}|_{\max} = 0.17$. Interestingly, this increases with $N$
($0.67$ for $N=20$), suggesting that certain collective features become energy-predictive in the
Wigner regime.
\item At intermediate $\omega$: Correlations are generally weaker, consistent with the ``competition
regime'' requiring complex, distributed representations.
\end{itemize}

To make these correlations more interpretable, recall that the pooled PINN feature vector used here is
$\rho_{\rm in}=[\bar\phi_0,\ldots,\bar\phi_4\;|\;\bar\psi_0,\ldots,\bar\psi_4\;|\;\langle r^2\rangle,\langle s_1\rangle]$,
where $\bar\phi_k$ are pooled single-particle features, $\bar\psi_k$ are pooled pair features,
$\langle r^2\rangle$ is the mean squared (scaled) radius, and $\langle s_1\rangle$ is a smooth short-range
radial summary (see \texttt{\_safe\_pair\_features} in the code).
Table~\ref{tab:energy_corr_topfeat} lists the single most energy-correlated channel per case.

\subsection{Explained variance in physical quantities}

To connect the learned representation to physical observables, we compute $R^2$ values for linear
regressions predicting various quantities from the PINN features:
\begin{itemize}
\item $R^2(\bar{r})$: mean radius prediction---consistently $> 0.94$ across all cases.
\item $R^2(\mathrm{var}(r))$: radius variance prediction---$> 0.4$ for $N \geq 6$.
\item $R^2(r_{\min})$: minimum pair distance prediction---more variable, stronger at intermediate $\omega$.
\end{itemize}

These high $R^2$ values confirm that the PINN features encode physically meaningful information about
the configuration geometry, not arbitrary correlates.

\section{Low-rank structure and efficient compression}
\label{sec:pc_ablation}

A key question for neural network wavefunctions is whether the learned representations admit efficient
low-rank approximations. We address this through principal component ablation studies, measuring how
reconstruction error decreases as we retain more PCA modes. Crucially, we compare against random
projection baselines to test whether the low-rank structure is meaningful or merely reflects low variance.

\subsection{Backflow field compression}

Table~\ref{tab:pc_ablation_dx} shows the relative reconstruction error for the backflow field $\Delta x$
as a function of retained principal components $k$, compared to random projections.

The results reveal remarkably efficient compression:
\begin{itemize}
\item For $N = 2$: Just 2 principal components capture the full backflow field to machine precision
($< 10^{-10}$). The effective rank $\mathrm{erank}(\Delta x) \approx 3.6$ confirms this low-dimensional structure.
\item For $N = 6$: The error drops from $\sim 92\%$ (1~PC) to $\sim 72\%$ (4~PCs) to essentially zero (12~PCs).
This matches the ambient dimension $2N = 12$, but the rapid decay shows that most variance
concentrates in fewer modes.
\item For $N = 12$: Similar decay pattern, reaching $\sim 61\%$ error at 12~PCs (out of ambient $2N = 24$).
\end{itemize}

Crucially, the PCA-based compression substantially outperforms random projections across all cases.
For $N=6$ at $\omega=0.1$, 4~PCs achieve $72\%$ relative error, while 4~random directions achieve $82\%$ error.
The gap persists and often widens at higher $k$. This confirms that the backflow field has genuine
low-rank structure aligned with meaningful physical modes, not just low variance.

\subsection{PINN representation compression}

Table~\ref{tab:pc_ablation_pinn} shows the analogous analysis for the PINN feature representation.
The compression is even more dramatic:
\begin{itemize}
\item The first principal component typically achieves mean absolute error (MAE) $< 0.5$, while
random projections give MAE $\sim 1$--$20$ depending on $(N, \omega)$.
\item By $k = 4$--$6$ PCs, the MAE drops to $< 0.1$ in most cases, while random projections remain
at MAE $\sim 0.3$--$5$.
\item The gap between PCA and random is enormous: for $(N=12, \omega=0.001)$, 4~PCs give MAE $= 0.5$
while 4~random directions give MAE $= 17.7$.
\end{itemize}

This confirms that the PINN learns a low-dimensional ``summary'' of the configuration that the CTNN
then expands into the high-dimensional backflow field. The compression efficiency also suggests that
reduced-rank approximations could provide computational savings without sacrificing accuracy.

\section{CTNN internal geometry: low intrinsic dimension but broad variance support}
\label{sec:ctnn_internal}

We now examine the internal structure of the CTNN backflow network itself. Table~\ref{tab:ctnn_idim_case}
reports intrinsic-dimension estimates for key network blocks: node embeddings, edge embeddings, edge updates,
node updates, and the final displacement head.

\subsection{Low intrinsic dimension in early layers}

The earliest CTNN blocks show remarkably low intrinsic dimension:
$\mathrm{idim}(\texttt{node\_embed}) \approx 1.3$--$1.5$ and
$\mathrm{idim}(\texttt{edge\_embed}) \approx 1.9$--$2.5$ across all $(N, \omega)$.

This reflects a fundamental constraint: the CTNN ingests low-dimensional geometric primitives
(2D coordinates, pairwise relative vectors), so its early representations cannot explore
more degrees of freedom than the input provides. The slight increase with $N$ reflects the
richer combinatorics of larger systems.

\subsection{Expansion in update layers}

The message-passing update layers show larger intrinsic dimensions:
$\mathrm{idim}(\texttt{edge\_update}) \approx 3.5$--$4.7$ and
$\mathrm{idim}(\texttt{node\_update}) \approx 3.5$--$4.7$.

The regime dependence is mild but consistent: intrinsic dimension peaks at $\omega \approx 0.1$
(the competition regime identified earlier), suggesting that the intermediate confinement regime
requires the most expressive internal representations.

\subsection{Low intrinsic dimension vs.\ high variance support}

A key interpretability finding is that low intrinsic dimension can coexist with high-dimensional
variance support. Table~\ref{tab:dimproxy_omegaavg} shows that while $\mathrm{idim}$ remains
$\mathcal{O}(1$--$5)$, the PCA-based metrics $d_{\rm PR}$ and $k_{95}$ are much larger:
$k_{95}(\texttt{edge\_update}) \approx 150$ and $k_{95}(\texttt{node\_update}) \approx 65$--$70$.

This is not a contradiction. A low-dimensional manifold can curve through many ambient dimensions,
requiring many PCA modes to capture its variance even though only a few intrinsic degrees of freedom
are active. The CTNN appears to exploit this: it learns representations that evolve along
low-dimensional trajectories in a high-dimensional ambient space, achieving expressivity
through nonlinear structure rather than high-dimensional spreading.


\section{Summary: what the network learns}
\label{sec:repgeom_summary}

Our analysis reveals a coherent picture of how neural network wavefunctions encode correlation physics:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Scaling with system size:} $\mathrm{erank}(\Delta x)$ and $\mathrm{idim}(\Delta x)$ grow strongly with $N$
(Table~\ref{tab:repgeom_Navg}), demonstrating that the learned backflow field is not a low-mode correction but a genuinely
high-dimensional correlated displacement field using $\sim 50$--$75\%$ of available degrees of freedom.

\item \textbf{Regime dependence:} The intermediate confinement $\omega\approx 0.1$ exhibits simultaneously increased
representation complexity in $\rho_{\rm in}$, elevated tangential fraction $f_{\rm tan}$, and larger near-field
amplification $A_{\rm nf}$ (Table~\ref{tab:repgeom_omegaavg}), consistent with a ``competition regime'' where neither
Fermi-liquid nor Wigner-molecule pictures suffice.

\item \textbf{Feature importance shifts with regime:} Pair-angle features ($\psi$) dominate at intermediate and strong
confinement, while absolute angular features ($\phi$) become important in the Wigner limit for larger systems
(Table~\ref{tab:feature_ablation}). This connects learned representations to physical intuition about correlation structure.

\item \textbf{Which pooled channels ``matter'' is measurable:} Energy correlations and gradient-based importances identify
which pooled feature families drive the readout in different regimes.
Strong confinement can be dominated by a single $\psi_k$ channel (Table~\ref{tab:energy_corr_topfeat}), while the overall
readout sensitivity often concentrates on the pair-feature family in larger systems (Table~\ref{tab:rho_weight_share}).

\item \textbf{Near-field emphasis:} $A_{\rm nf}>1$ over broad regimes indicates systematic amplification of backflow
corrections during close encounters (Table~\ref{tab:repgeom_casegrid}), consistent with correlation/cusp physics.

\item \textbf{Dominant backflow mode is typically collective:} PC1 participation metrics indicate that the leading backflow
pattern is spread across many particles for $N\ge 6$, with only mild localization in some strongly confined cases
(Table~\ref{tab:pc1_participation}).

\item \textbf{Low-rank structure enables compression:} Both the PINN representation and the backflow field admit efficient
PCA compression, substantially outperforming random projections (Tables~\ref{tab:pc_ablation_dx}--\ref{tab:pc_ablation_pinn}).
This suggests that the learned corrections, while high-dimensional, lie on structured low-dimensional manifolds.

\item \textbf{The CTNN performs nonlinear expansion:} The small linear CKA between $\rho_{\rm in}$ and $\Delta x$,
combined with the low intrinsic dimension but high variance support in CTNN layers, indicates that the backflow network
performs substantial nonlinear processing rather than linear readout.

\item \textbf{Backflow can directly improve energy:} A direct MCMC probe on fixed PINNs shows that adding backflow tends to
lower the energy for $N\ge 6$ at moderate/strong confinement, linking the geometric and feature-based diagnostics to a concrete
variational benefit (Table~\ref{tab:bf_energy_delta_mcmc}).
\end{enumerate}

These findings support the view that neural network wavefunctions learn physically meaningful
representations of electron correlation, with interpretable structure that adapts to the
correlation regime.


\chapter{Wigner regime: real-space ordering and mode-based validation}
\label{subsec:Wigner}

Having established energetic accuracy in the benchmarked regimes (Table~\ref{tab:energies}), we now turn to a
structural question: \emph{what configuration do the electrons adopt in real space as confinement weakens and Coulomb
repulsion dominates?}  In a finite, rotationally symmetric trap the appropriate object is a \emph{Wigner molecule}:
electrons localize in \emph{relative} coordinates (rings/shells with polygon-like angular correlations), while the
entire pattern may rotate. Consequently, lab-frame angles can appear nearly uniform even when strong internal order
is present. We therefore combine: (i) one-body density visualization, (ii) a label-invariant shell topology, (iii)
rotation-registered bond-orientational order (internal order disentangled from lab-frame pinning), (iv) shell radii
and separation gaps, (v) a pair-statistics validation isolating angular correlations beyond shelling, and (vi) a
mode-alignment diagnostic testing whether the sampled fluctuations are consistent with structured small oscillations
around a localized configuration.

$\omega=1.0$ and weak confinement $\omega=0.001$ for $N=2,6,12,20$. At $\omega=1.0$ the density remains compact and

\paragraph{Rotation-registered angular order (internal order vs.\ lab-frame pinning).}
Shell occupancies capture \emph{radial} localization but not whether particles on a ring exhibit polygon-like
\emph{relative} angular correlations. Because the Hamiltonian is rotationally symmetric, the correct diagnostic is
a registered (co-rotating) frame: strong internal order may coexist with a diffusing global orientation. For shell
$s$ with $n_s$ particles we compute
\begin{equation}
\psi_{n_s}^{\mathrm{reg}}(t)
=
\left|
\frac{1}{n_s}\sum_{j\in s} e^{i n_s \theta^{\mathrm{reg}}_j(t)}
\right|,
\qquad
\Phi_{s,\mathrm{reg}}=\langle \psi_{n_s}^{\mathrm{reg}} \rangle_t,
\end{equation}
and summarize internal order by
$C_{\tilde{}}\_{\mathrm{global}}=\sum_s \Phi_{s,\mathrm{reg}}$ (which can exceed unity when multiple shells are ordered).
We additionally report a lab-frame analogue $C_{\mathrm{global}}=\sum_s P_{s,\mathrm{lab}}$, which should be interpreted
as a \emph{pinning/rotation-timescale} diagnostic on the sampled time window.

Table~\ref{tab:ang_order_key} shows that internal polygonal order is strongest in the Wigner regime and collapses
rapidly when topology collapses.
For $N=12$, the Wigner case $\omega=10^{-3}$ exhibits \emph{two simultaneously ordered shells} and reaches
$C_{\tilde{}}\_{\mathrm{global}}=1.0684\pm0.0043$, while already at $\omega=10^{-2}$ (after the transition to $(1,11)$)
the internal order drops to $0.2755\pm0.0012$ and remains $\approx 0.268$ at $\omega=1$.
For $N=20$, $\omega=10^{-3}$ again shows multi-shell internal order with $C_{\tilde{}}\_{\mathrm{global}}=0.7909\pm0.0027$,
whereas the collapsed $(1,19)$ states have $C_{\tilde{}}\_{\mathrm{global}}\simeq 0.203$ at $\omega\ge 10^{-2}$.
In all cases, $C_{\mathrm{global}}$ is much smaller than $C_{\tilde{}}\_{\mathrm{global}}$, consistent with weak
lab-frame pinning and substantial global rotation over the sampled trajectories.

\paragraph{Mode-alignment diagnostic (are the fluctuations ``molecule-like''?).}
A Wigner molecule is not merely a static ring pattern: if electrons are localized around a classical equilibrium,
their residual motion should resemble structured small oscillations (collective distortions) rather than featureless
fluid-like wandering. We test this by comparing the empirical covariance $C$ (center-of-mass removed) to the Hessian
$H$ of the corresponding classical equilibrium configuration (trap + Coulomb, scaled to the same $\omega$). If
fluctuations are organized by classical normal modes, $C$ and $H$ should be approximately compatible (near joint
diagonalization) and a nontrivial fraction of variance should lie in the span of the softest modes. The diagnostics
in Table~\ref{tab:classical_mode_alignment} support this interpretation: for $N=6$ the commutator norm is smallest at
$\omega=10^{-3}$ and increases with confinement, while for $N=12$ and $N=20$ the commutator norms remain small and
overlap weights show a reproducible concentration of variance into a soft-mode subspace even though the fluctuation
space is high-dimensional.

\paragraph{Pair-statistics validation (angular order beyond shelling).}
Shell topology captures the dominant radial organization, but it does not by itself guarantee polygon-like angular
correlations. We therefore compare the empirical global pair-distance histogram to two shell-based generative models
sharing the \emph{same} shell assignment: (i) a polygon model (regular $n_s$-gons per shell with optimal phase alignment)
and (ii) a uniform-angle model (same shell radii but no angular order). Table~\ref{tab:shell_pair_model_validation}
shows that the polygon model systematically outperforms the uniform-angle baseline, and that the gain
$\Delta\cos=\cos_{\mathrm{poly}}-\cos_{\mathrm{uni}}$ is strongest in the weakest trap and decreases with $\omega$,
isolating a genuine angular-correlation signal beyond radial shelling alone.

\paragraph{Discussion (what the combined diagnostics establish).}
Across $N=2,6,12,20$ we observe a consistent and internally validated Wigner-to-denser structural crossover as $\omega$
decreases. The one-body densities show dramatic spatial expansion and ring formation at $\omega=10^{-3}$.
The shell topology reveals \emph{discrete} finite-$N$ Wigner patterns at $\omega=10^{-3}$ and sharp collapses at
$\omega=10^{-2}$ for $N=12$ and $N=20$. The shell radii and separation gaps provide a quantitative real-space
counterpart: in the Wigner regime the shells are widely separated and stable, while their radii and gaps shrink
rapidly as confinement strengthens. Rotation-registered bond-orientational order demonstrates strong polygon-like
internal angular localization in the Wigner regime (including multi-shell ordering for $N=12$ and $N=20$), while the
lab-frame measures remain comparatively small and should be interpreted as pinning/rotation-timescale diagnostics
rather than internal order. The Lindemann measures rise strongly with confinement, giving an independent signature of
progressive melting. Finally, the pair-distance validation confirms that angular correlations contribute explanatory
power beyond shelling, with the largest gains at weak confinement, and the mode-alignment analysis adds mechanistic
support: in the low-$\omega$ regime the fluctuations retain structured, collective content compatible with
small-oscillation physics around localized configurations, as expected for Wigner molecules in a finite trap.
